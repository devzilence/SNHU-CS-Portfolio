## CS-250: Software Development Lifecycle (2026)

### Artifact: Sprint Review and Retrospective

This artifact is my Sprint Review and Retrospective from the SNHU Travel project. It brings together the Agile and Scrum practices I used throughout the course, including user stories, test cases, sprint planning, and reflections on team roles.

### Essential Questions

#### How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating "user stories" help with this?

I interpret user needs by focusing on what the user is trying to do and why that matters to them. In the SNHU Travel project, that meant thinking about different kinds of users, like someone who wants to quickly view the top five destinations, a returning customer who wants personalized recommendations, or a traveler looking for deals that match their budget and style. Breaking those needs into user stories helped make the project feel more practical because each story focused on a real user goal instead of just a feature.

User stories also helped me stay focused on the value behind the feature. They made it easier to think from the user's point of view and connect requirements to something the team could actually build and test. Once I added acceptance criteria, it became much clearer what the feature needed to do and whether it was actually complete.

#### How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?

I approach developing programs by starting with smaller pieces that work and then building from there. This project showed me that it is better to get one feature working first, like the top five destinations list, before moving into more complex features like personalized recommendations. That approach makes it easier to show progress, get feedback, and adjust before too much time is spent going in the wrong direction.

The Agile processes I want to keep using in the future are sprint planning, maintaining a prioritized backlog, daily standups, and retrospectives. I like that Agile makes room for change and keeps the team focused on what matters most in the moment. Daily updates help surface blockers early, while retrospectives give the team time to reflect on what worked and what needs improvement.

#### What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?

To me, being a good team member in software development means being honest, dependable, and clear in communication. One of the biggest things I took from this project was that it is better to speak up early when something is taking longer than expected or when a requirement is confusing. That gives the team time to adjust instead of letting a problem grow until the end of the sprint.

I also learned that being a good team member means understanding how your role connects to everyone else's role. The product owner focuses on priorities, the Scrum Master helps remove blockers, and developers and testers need to be realistic about what can be finished in a sprint. When everyone communicates well and does their part, the team works much better and the Agile process is more effective.
